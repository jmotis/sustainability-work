When I read about the cure-alls, I wondered how many of them actually worked. Were they just the sixteenth-century equivalent of Victorian patent medicines? Or were they just placebos? I found the author’s inclusion of societal norms surrounding death to be rather interesting. Obviously, the poison trials had to have a veneer of respectability by sixteenth century standards, but by modern standards, the practice stands out as barbaric. When the author brought up COVID in her introduction, I was reminded of vaccine trials. Unlike the poison trials, however, vaccine trials work off the principle of voluntary participation. They also collect data from a broad cross-section of society as a whole. The poison trials, by contrast, were involuntary and represented a small portion of society. That might be because of the fact that these poison trials were conducted for the benefit of aristocrats and royalty. Poisoning was not a problem in everyday Renaissance life, with the sole exception of lead poisoning, which would have trickled in via the water supply in urban areas.
