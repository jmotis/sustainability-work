]]> By: William Dunn https://dev.jessicaotis.com/2023/04/23/review-john-m-dixons-the-enlightenment-of-cadwallader-colden-empire-science-and-intellectual-culture-in-british-new-york/#comment-78 Wed, 26 Apr 2023 01:43:50 +0000 https://dev.jessicaotis.com/?p=638#comment-78 Power, politics, philosophy, and a man on the make. Was Colden even interested in science, or was it just one more status game? Dixon has introduced us to the political anthill that early 18C British American strivers fought so hard to dominate. Colden’s father and grandfather were 17C Presbyterians, and by the rhetoric of the time probably considered Whigs, and radical ones at that. By the time Colden graduated from U. Edinburgh, the Presbyterians were the dominant group in Scotland and part of the striving middle class. Colden’s efforts to become a gentleman and a part of the landed gentry led him to “upstate” New York. And therein hangs the tale that Dixon tells well.

What complicates the “is it science?” query is that Colden has so many motives and interests. Is knowledge acquired along the way? Certainly. Is Colden a philosopher? That is a more challenging question. Dixon shows that Colden was smart, ambitious, and a careful political “trimmer.” So, when Colden develops a theory of matter to explain gravitation more satisfactorily than Newton it is challenging to assess his work: is it an original insight, or an effort to gain prominence in European intellectual circles, or both, or something else? As our reviewers have noted, Colder appears to have done little experimentation.

By the end of the tale, Colden is being attacked by Livingston and his new-model Reflectors. These New Whigs, of course, were the wave of a future that Colden inadvertently helped to create but didn’t live to see. Colden had become a successful and loyal subject of the Crown, a Royalist with Tory views, and a proponent of an increasingly ignored view of harmonious pursuit of enlightenment through reasoning by worthy elites. Was he an exponent of a kind of special “Tory” science? Did he view “enlightenment” as an improving, Tory project? Or was Colden just another striver on the anthill?

Dixon’s book is unsettling in a very interesting way. His Colden is no hero, but neither is he a villain — rather, he is a tragic figure. Seeking wealth and status he gains both only to be vilified for what he has accomplished. Seeking intellectual renown he gains access to the Republic of Letters, has his moment of fame, only to be dismissed as unworthy. In telling Colden’s complicated tale Dixon illuminates British and colonial politics. We also see the effects of some of the religious and philosophical currents circulating in elite circles. But mostly we see religion, philosophy, theory, and experiment caught in constant political striving.
