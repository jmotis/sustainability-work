]]> By: William Dunn https://dev.jessicaotis.com/2023/04/10/andres-i-prietos-missionary-scientists-jesuit-science-in-spanish-south-america-1570-1810/#comment-65 Tue, 11 Apr 2023 16:22:15 +0000 https://dev.jessicaotis.com/?p=620#comment-65 Your review notes the competing objectives of the actors in this place and time and emphasizes the ability of the Jesuit priests to adapt and to pursue their own objectives. This book provides insights into the Jesuits’ developing sense of their organizational mission, their ongoing competition with royal administrators, their efforts to understand native culture and beliefs, and their ability to develop effective administrative and theological innovations in response. This intellectual history seems to me the most valuable contribution of the book.

The book shows that in the late 16C and continuing throughout the 17C, Jesuit priests, among the most highly-educated men of the time, participated in the Republic of Letters and some had an interest in cosmography and natural history. A telling detail is the episode that Prieto describes where a local craftsman is able to create a high quality telescope from local materials. This suggests: the priest who commissioned the telescope had a fundamental understanding of the principles involved and could direct the construction; or the local craftsman had already acquired the necessary understanding, perhaps by working with another telescope. Either alternative shows that technology spread quickly.

Prieto’s history is incomplete. As you note, interesting cultural and anthropological questions are left unanswered. The competition between Galenic and shamanistic “medicine” is described from the point of view of the Jesuits. It would be interesting to analyze the struggle through the eyes of a shaman. The book only nods to the reception of the naturalist observations sent by the American Jesuits to Europe. The Jesuits enter the colonial story in media res; earlier missionaries’ (Dominicans, ?) efforts and accomplishments created expectations and possibilities that the Jesuits inherited. Still, Prieto has given us a useful intellectual history of one of the most remote parts of the early modern world.

I enjoyed this book because, as you mentioned, it goes in depth into the Catholic religious motivations in scientific inquiry. It was a different lens on religion and science that we haven’t read about in depth yet, and I appreciated having the comparison to other books this semester which focus more on science through a protestant perspective. It was fascinating to read about how the Jesuit missionaries found, for lack of a better word, “loopholes” in their efforts to understanding the flora and fauna of Latin America, as well as the medicinal and social practices of the indigenous populations. By loopholes I mean that they found a way to produce scientific knowledge, gain indigenous information, and learn about the specific uses of local practices through the guise of Christianity and spreading Christian work (or by gaining new knowledge for the sake of Christianity). One thing I would like to discuss more in class is the use of confessionals as a way the Jesuits gained secret or ritual medicinal knowledge from indigenous peoples they were attempting to evangelize. I think this again brings up the discussions we’ve had about consent in early modern science. What I found interesting about this book was the fine line that missionaries walked between helping and harming, exploitation, and knowledge exchange, etc. One aspect of the book that I think could be improved is a lack of visual aids and descriptions of the flora and fauna, the indigenous peoples, and indigenous cultures that were being discussed. Overall, an enjoyable book.
