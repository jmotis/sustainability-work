I liked your paragraph on the “spectacle” aspect. The fact that Louis eventually lost interest in the project puts more weight on the possibility that these demonstrations were predominantly power displays. Sort of a cross between a modern TED talk or Royal Institution Youtube lecture and the entertainment value of a public execution. It’s interesting that this era produced the first zoo where animals were organized on the basis of species, yet the dissection project did not really lay much groundwork for evolutionary theory. This “spectacular” quality, as well as the accessibility of anatomical drawings (which did not require much knowledge of Latin for comprehension) goes a long way toward explaining why anatomy became the first “popular” science, in my view.
