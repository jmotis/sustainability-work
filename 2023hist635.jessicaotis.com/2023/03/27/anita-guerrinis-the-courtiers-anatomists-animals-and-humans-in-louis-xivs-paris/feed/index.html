At the beginning of the book, I was skeptical about how much I would enjoy it since I’m not a fan of reading about dissection and vivisection (more akin to torture), but I was surprised at how interesting it ended up being. In particular, one aspect of the book that was most fascinating to me was the relationship between the Academy and the King. This goes along with the recurring theme of patronage in our class discussions, but the dynamic between academics/scientists and the monarchy in France is unique to what we have discussed before. I appreciated your point about the abundance of evidence the author gives while simultaneously jumping between multiple historical figures, and I completely agree with you. At times, the amount of information she presents is a bit overwhelming. Overall, though, I enjoyed reading this book and its emphasis on the natural history side of early modern science. In class, I would be interested in a discussion about the ethics of dissection and vivisection – especially because these anatomists sometimes got their specimens in unsavory ways, like graverobbing. The aspect of consent and the ethics behind these kinds of experiments reminded me of The Poison Trials, and I’m interested in seeing what everyone else thinks. Nice book review!
