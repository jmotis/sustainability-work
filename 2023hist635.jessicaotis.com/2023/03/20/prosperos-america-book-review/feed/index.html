I appreciated your inclusion of the transatlantic scope of the work. It is interesting to me what knowledge traditions are carried over in colonization, it never occurred to me that alchemical knowledge would count among said transfer. I thoroughly enjoyed reading about the inclusion alchemy in the corpus of science during this period. I think some of the works we have read so far for the class have treated alchemy as a somewhat esoteric aspect of the colonial centuries, Poison Trials being one that comes to mind. It seems that the discipline of science in our modern times has strived to thoroughly divorce itself from its mystic origins, which is a shame because, as we can see in Woodward’s work, magic and mysticism were integral to early modern scientific thought. While I agree with you that the book circumscribes any easy historiographical classification, parts of the book may have benefitted from closer analysis, particularly when we consider the challenge of firmly placing this work into a subdiscipline of history. If this is a cultural history, why not include more analysis about the cultural diffusion in alchemical practices between indigenous and European bodies of knowledge. If this is a public history, then the link between neoplatonism, New England culture, and English culture could have used more analysis (at least in my opinion). Overall, I think Woodward did an excellent job of placing alchemical science within the context of formative New England culture.

]]> By: Alexa Shipes https://dev.jessicaotis.com/2023/03/20/prosperos-america-book-review/#comment-44 Tue, 21 Mar 2023 15:50:28 +0000 https://dev.jessicaotis.com/?p=587#comment-44 I really liked your review. The author’s point about John Winthrop Jr. being extremely tolerant was a pleasant surprise, especially given the time period in question. I wonder if the belief in things like alchemy (i.e. the belief that anything is possible) ultimately led to a more tolerant society. The providential knowledge that Winthrop and his contemporaries sought might have played a role in jumpstarting the Age of Enlightenment and all of its musings on freedom and democracy. The belief that anything is possible led men like Winthrop to look for lead deposits in Massachusetts and attempt to treat a well-known pastor’s father for (what we know as) ALS. It also led Sir Francis Bacon to publish Novum Organum, which laid out the scientific method still followed today, and drove Thomas Harriot to improve navigational methods to such a degree that the Apollo 13 astronauts used them to return to Earth.
