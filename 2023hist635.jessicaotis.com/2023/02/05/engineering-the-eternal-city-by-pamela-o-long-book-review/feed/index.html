My favorite thing about it is where you contrast the physical vs. the social and cultural sides of Rome. For example, I like how you contrast the physical situation of the city in the middle of a flood plain, requiring ambitious engineering projects, vs. the promotion of the Rome in guidebooks and maps “as a beautiful Renaissance city.” I think the physical vs. social is an important theme of the book that kept coming to mind as I was reading.

You also write that you think there is too much of a focus on the social side of things in chapter seven. I wonder what you think about Long’s treatment of cultural or social history, if either of those are the right label, in the rest of the book. Personally, looking back, I find her discussions of physical infrastructure and engineering projects more memorable than her discussion of politics and social life.

Still, I want to hold judgement until our discussion in class tomorrow. When I think of a city as famous as Rome, even though I’ve never had the luck to visit it, beautiful architecture and larger-than-life figures, like Popes or Roman emperors, automatically come to mind. Delicious food, too, but that’s besides the point here. Anyway, it was refreshing to read Long’s discussion of the mundane details of municipal government, like how the Capitoline Council worked and the political bickering between Romans when they were trying to repair aqueducts.

Whatever decision I make about Long’s book, reading your review emphasized interesting themes about urban history and the relationship between human beings and the environment in which they live.

I especially liked your point about the weaving together of the architectural efforts of Rome in this period with the themes of culture and social interconnectedness. Long’s Chapter Seven does seem a bit out of place, but I believe that the chapter was her attempt to incorporate the societal expectations and obligations as well as cultural/political assertion of power by the popes of the time, hence the “Reforming the Streets” title of the section. I think this was the part that most fascinated me about the reading as Rome remained from ancient times through this period a city somewhat unique in its social webs and client-patronage mechanisms. I agree, too, that this book certainly fills in a gap in knowledge in showing how Rome became a modern city, but do feel that the book’s narrow timeframe and topic as well as its careful focus on the finer minutiae may not be the best introduction to the period of Roman/Italian history more generally. I am eager to see what others think.
