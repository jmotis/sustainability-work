There were two points you made in your criticism of Delbourgo that reminded me of my reaction to his book. First, regarding your point about him missing the forest for the trees, until I settled into Delbourgo’s writing style by about chapter two, I kept being distracted by tangents away from Sloane’s story. I had the impression by that point in the book that too much of the narrative covered the broad historical background for Sloane’s world. That kind of detail is important, but I felt like there should have been less of it. Then again, that just might be the way I remember it.

Nevertheless, even the tangents were interesting, especially the parts about the history of slavery and the transmission of food from Africa to Jamaica. _Collecting_the_World_ is part environmental history. I think Delbourgo is a good writer. If I would have run across this book outside of this class, I would have enjoyed reading it.

Your other point that I liked was about the lack of discussion of postcolonial theory. I agree that Delbourgo should have used the opportunity to address issues having to do with colonialism (or addressed them more than he did). A talented writer like Delbourgo should be able to weave some discussion of theory into his book in a readable and indirect way. I suppose that’s like a doctor giving a patient bitter medicine with enough sugar to make the medicine go down easily.

Now, I want to try to remember a historian who is a good example of weaving theory into history. The first one that comes to mind is Michel Rolph-Trouillo and _Silencing_the_Past_. At least, to me when I read that book last semester it seemed like a good example of combining history and theory. I am curious if you have any examples of historians who handle theory well.

One cannot separate Sloane’s collection from the circumstances of the time period in which it was gathered. Even today, the British Museum is at the heart of controversy over looted artifacts, many of which were acquired in the Victorian era, a full century after Sloane’s death and the foundation of the British Museum. Sloane’s collection did not have any glamourous Assyrian or Greek artifacts, nor did it have any flashy pieces that advertised Britain’s imperial power, like the Benin Bronzes. However, it is not immune to scrutiny over the circumstances in which the objects were acquired. The fact that Sloane had time to go around Jamaica looking for butterflies shows that slavery permeated every aspect of life on the island. His friends in Jamaica were all plantation owners who benefitted from the exploitation of enslaved men, women, and children. When Sloane wished to create a public museum, his executors took steps to keep out the general public and restrict entrance to the wealthy.
