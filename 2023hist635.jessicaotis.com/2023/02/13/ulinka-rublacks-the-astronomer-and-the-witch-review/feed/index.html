Her explanation of the role of older women, especially widows in the community, and Katrinia’s nonconformity to these ideals leading suspicions against her. The way Rublack refutes common myths about accusations of witchcraft in German communities. She explained that accusations were not used as a form of scapegoating in struggling rural communities; instead, they were concentrated in bigger towns. I also appreciated how Rublack described how Kepler’s religious convictions affected his academic life. Nevertheless, like you, I found Rublack’s work a well-written, informative, and enthralling read.

I appreciate your focus on Kepler’s expertise serving him in other scholarly avenues besides astronomy and mathematics. I was impressed at the stubbornness of Kepler and Katharina, and how their relation to German Lutheranism innately defined their character and responses to the trial. I agree with you that this is a microhistory, though I disagree that it is too large in scope. My understanding of microhistory as a method entails a thorough archival examination about a singular event, and through this fervent examination, connections to phenomena occurring over a short span of time are connected. This is in contrast to a history which aims to identify causality. If we apply the keyhole metaphor to Rublack’s work, I think it meets the criteria for a true microhistory, though I agree with you that the organization of the work could be a bit clearer (The Dream chapter seemed a bit out of place).

I admit, that while reading the book I was far more concerned with Kepler’s thought processes than I was with the trial and the cultural belief in witches. Though this work is less about the “scientific revolution” than the previous few we read, I think it has remarkable implications for this class. On page 287, Rublack asserts that Kepler combined various knowledge making methods to reach his conclusions: mathematical certainty, speculative imagination, and logically causal explanations. In short, there was a faith-based component to Kepler’s calculations, faith in god and faith in his capacity to reason. We see these dual fates in his legal defense of his mother. This method is the inverse of the Aristotelian style, Kepler proceeds from specific mathematical certainties to general comments about the nature of the planets, and natural philosophy. While this is inductive reasoning as opposed to the deductive reasoning of the Aristotelian school, the general processes of knowledge making follow the same principles, albeit reversed.

Again, great review, looking forward to the discussion!
